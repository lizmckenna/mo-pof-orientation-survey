Special education

Seven of you are raising kids with IEPs, 504s, or diagnoses.

It came out in different places on the form. For a lot of you, fighting for your own kid is how you learned to fight at all.

George was recently diagnosed with ADHD in the fall, prior to which we were getting a lot of messages from teachers and administrators about his behavior in class. Since we have figured out his diagnosis, medication, and other behavioral supports he has been having a much more positive classroom experience. We have had some frustrations with getting a 504 plan in place, as we've been denied after a long evaluation process. David Tremaine · Hale Cook, KCPS
8th grade daughter with IEP, mixed experiences primarily related to who is IEP case manager each year as well as grade principal and school counselor. 6th grade son, requested IEP and was denied after evaluation; his overall experience has varied greatly depending on his specific teacher. Melissa Miller · Kirkwood School District
One of my twins has autism and ADHD. He has really struggled at school and I have felt like I have had to be an advocate for him a few times. He currently has an IEP. Sierra Kilpatrick · Davidson Elementary, NKC
He was diagnosed with autism and ADHD at the age of 6. He was later diagnosed with anxiety. He struggles in a traditional school setting, especially given the lack of support for special education services. Judy Young · Independence · MO PTA Advocacy Chair
The small group that keeps showing up

You kept telling us about the same few parents doing all of it.

From Francis Howell to Border Star Montessori, you described the same thing: a handful of people doing everything, already tired. You're here because you're one of them, and because you already know that can't keep working.

What I see repeatedly, through my work with Missouri Citizens for Safe Communities, parent club, Girl Scouts, athletic boosters, and beyond, is the same small group of parents showing up every single time. Those people are invaluable, and they are burning out. That's not sustainable, and it's not a foundation you can build on. Organizing parents into leaders changes that dynamic. The skills learned don't stay siloed in one organization, they transfer across school groups, across causes, and across the community. Stephanie Rittgers · Francis Howell · Co-President, MOCSC
It's my first year at Border Star and while I've loved being involved in the PTA, I'm surprised by how much is being done by so few people on the exec board. I'm curious (suspicious??) that if we start reaching out to people with purpose there will be many who'd like to participate who either a) don't know all that PTA is doing, b) don't know how their skills or preferences could help, or c) need to feel invited in vs volunteering. Ellen Schwartze · Border Star Montessori, KCPS

Teachers

Teacher retention is the thing keeping you up at night.

Teacher retention. This is the issue that keeps me up at night. My oldest had to rearrange her schedule in high school to work around teacher shortages, particularly in the upper sciences. AP Chemistry was especially difficult; she eventually got the class she needed, but the teacher who taught her has since left the district. Within four years, there will have been three different teachers in that role, and there is no guarantee a qualified AP Chemistry teacher will be in place when my youngest is slated to take the course next fall. Five years ago, open teaching positions in FHSD attracted hundreds of applicants. Today, many postings receive only a handful. Stephanie Rittgers · Francis Howell
I want to see public education funding protected, see teacher pay increase, and improve the employment of teachers in general. We don't have a lack of teachers, we have a lack of educators who want to work in a public school setting because of the challenges it brings. Judy Young · Independence
I want to see the school (and the district) have more money to serve children and families the way they deserve, and I want to see teachers earn a higher pay that aligns with their great necessity and impact on our society. LaNee Bridewell · Lincoln College Prep, KCPS

What leadership means to you

Almost nobody said "being in charge."

We asked all 17 of you what leadership means. Hardly anyone described it as being the person out front. You talked about holding space, stepping back, handing things off, making room for someone else to try.

Leadership means creating a safe and courageous container for individual and communal transformation and liberation through support, collaborative learning, and empowerment. David Tremaine
Leadership is elevating and organizing the work of other people who have skills and abilities that contribute to the overall success of the organization and mission. Judy Young
Leadership means serving those you're leading. Being both their support and guide. Sarah Laub
Walking the walk, knowing when to follow, not being too good for any individual task, keeping people connected to the purpose and "why" behind their original involvement in whatever the situation is. Benjamin Roesler
Leadership, to me, means stepping into a role that scares you a little, and doing it anyway. It means taking a leap, doing the hard work of becoming a better version of yourself, and then turning around and giving others permission to do the same. Stephanie Rittgers
It's less about being visible and "in charge" and more about creating opportunities for other people to succeed. LaNee Bridewell
Hope

Your hope is specific. It points at wins that already happened.

I had the privilege of speaking with Molly Fleming about what Parents for KC Kids accomplished last year, and it moved me. Hearing about how an organized group of parents was able to show up, make noise, and actually move the needle reminded me of what is possible when people are mobilized with purpose and skill. What gave me hope wasn't just the outcome. It was the model. It wasn't one charismatic leader who made it happen. It was a group of trained, connected parents who knew how to organize, how to recruit, and how to sustain momentum. That's replicable. Stephanie Rittgers · St. Charles County
Seeing the results of many special elections throughout the country gives me hope that even in a state like Missouri we can find and mobilize enough people to make a difference and fight special interests that often harm the majority of Missourians. Melissa Miller
Stories from the Civil Rights movement and the wins I've seen that have come out of more recent grassroots organizing, everything from raising minimum wage, KCPS families voting to pass the bond, Target boycott, etc. LaNee Bridewell
History. Every progressive step we've made through out the last 250 yrs was achieved through organizing. Sarah Laub
If you had a magic wand

We asked for one thing. You gave us ten.

Almost nobody picked just one. Schools are where you're standing right now, but you're also writing about housing, healthcare, reparations, income inequality, unifying your community, and making neighborhoods walkable. Nobody here is only about schools.

All of my family, friends, and neighbors with children would have them enrolled in their local public school, the state would fully fund public education, there would be confidence in the school district to respond to the needs of the community, and people would have a renewed sense that their primary responsibility is not to themselves or their personal finances, but to the wellness of others and their community as a whole. David Tremaine
Housing for all, Healthcare for all, No Hungry stomachs, Healthy food sources within walking distance, support classes for caregivers, low to free cost childcare, low to free cost trade schools, etc. AJ Johnson · Foreign Language Academy, KCPS
More equitable and gainful economic opportunity. I really believe this is at the core of every other societal ill. Here in the northeast there are high rates of addiction and substance abuse, property crime and SA, human trafficking and narcotics trafficking, both of which come with violence. People with options, people with something to lose, they don't start doing meth and fentanyl; that's why those overdoses are called "deaths of despair." Benjamin Roesler · Garfield Elementary
Reparations Free healthcare and food for all Unify our community More minority women in power Infrastructure that prioritizes people over cars Equal access for every child A less homogenized community
What you asked us

Your questions are what we owe you answers to.

  • "What are the ongoing responsibilities and expectations after the fellowship ends? Where is the funding for this program coming from? What are the metrics to determine whether the overall program is successful? What is the long term vision?" Melissa Miller
  • "What is the hope for the organization after the Fellowship is complete?" Sierra Kilpatrick
  • "How are you measuring the starting point of each fellow's community? (If you want an increase of 50%, what's that starting #.) Do you expect the summer to help or hinder the goals? Are we tracking hours literally or going on vibes?" Ellen Schwartze
  • "I'm curious what this will look like in action on a weekly basis, but I also get that might have to be something I figure out as we go." Sarah Laub
  • "Will there be any financial support to the candidates that are chosen for travel to Columbia?" AJ Johnson
  • "What if I fail to achieve the desired goal in the specified timeframe? Despite the successes I hinted at, I have had several setbacks and failures as well." Benjamin Roesler
  • "When do we start?" David Tremaine

Most of these fall in three piles: what happens after these three months, how we'll know it worked, and Benjamin's, which is what if I'm not enough. We'll spend real time on all three this weekend.
